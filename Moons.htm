Moon:

an object that orbits a planet or something else that is not a star. Besides planets, moons can circle dwarf planets, large asteroids, and other bodies.

Luna AKA: The Mooon

The Moon, Earth's only natural satellite, orbits 384,400 km away, presenting the same face due to gravitational forces. Its lunar day matches the lunar month at 29.5 Earth days.

Phobos

Phobos is the larger of Mars' two natural satellites, discovered in 1877 by astronomer Asaph Hall. It is named after the Greek god of fear and panic, who is the son of Ares and the twin brother of Deimos, the other moon of Mars.

Deimos

Deimos is the smaller and outer of Mars' two moons, with a mean radius of 6.2 km and an orbital period of 30.3 hours. Positioned 23,460 km from Mars, it is farther away compared to its counterpart, Phobos.

Europa

Europa, or Jupiter II, is the smallest of the four Galilean moons orbiting Jupiter, and the sixth-closest to the planet of all the 95 known moons of Jupiter. It is also the sixth-largest moon in the Solar System.

Io

Io, or Jupiter I, is the innermost and third-largest of the four Galilean moons of the planet Jupiter

Ganymede

Ganymede, or Jupiter III, is the largest and most massive natural satellite of Jupiter as well as the largest in the Solar System, being a planetary-mass moon. It is the largest Solar System object without an atmosphere, despite being the only moon in the Solar System with a substantial magnetic field.

Callisto

Callisto, or Jupiter IV, is the second-largest moon of Jupiter, after Ganymede. In the Solar System, it is the third-largest moon after Ganymede and Saturn's largest moon Titan, and as large as the smallest planet Mercury, though only about a third as massive.

Titan

Titan is the largest moon of Saturn and the second-largest in the Solar System, larger than any of the dwarf planets of the Solar System.

Dione

Dione, also designated Saturn IV, is the fourth-largest moon of Saturn. Its trailing hemisphere is marked by large ice cliffs called chasmata and is also darkened compared to the leading hemisphere.

Mimas

Mimas, also known as Saturn I, is a moon of Saturn with the second-largest crater in the Solar System, named Herschel. This crater, measuring 139 kilometers across, is about one-third of Mimas's average diameter and is thought to have formed from a highly energetic impact event.

Rhea

Rhea is Saturn's second-largest moon and the ninth-largest in the Solar System, boasting a surface area similar to Australia. Notably, it is the smallest Solar System object where precise measurements confirm a shape consistent with hydrostatic equilibrium.

Titania

Titania, also designated Uranus III, is the largest of the moons of Uranus and the eighth largest moon in the Solar System at a diameter of 1,578 kilometres, with a surface area comparable to that of Australia.

Miranda

Miranda, also designated Uranus V, is the smallest and innermost of Uranus's five round satellites. It was discovered by Gerard Kuiper on 16 February 1948, at McDonald Observatory in Texas, and named after Miranda from William Shakespeare's play The Tempest.

Ariel

Ariel is the fourth-largest of the 27 known moons of Uranus. Ariel orbits and rotates in the equatorial plane of Uranus, which is almost perpendicular to the orbit of Uranus and so has an extreme seasonal cycle.

Triton

Triton is the largest natural satellite of the planet Neptune and was the first Neptunian moon to be discovered, on October 10, 1846, by English astronomer William Lassell. It is the only large moon in the Solar System with a retrograde orbit, an orbit in the direction opposite to its planet's rotation.

Proteus

Proteus, also known as Neptune VIII, is the second-largest Neptunian moon and Neptune's largest inner satellite. Discovered by the Voyager 2 spacecraft in 1989, it is named after Proteus, the shape-changing sea god of Greek mythology.
